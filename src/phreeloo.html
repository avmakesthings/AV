PHREE LOO

Interaction design, Prototype design, Graphic design

A low-tech toilet prototype that uses the ‘safe sludge’ methodology to inactivate pathogens in the sanitation value chain, for the safe and sustainable management of human waste.

The Problem

In 2011, the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation introduced the Reinvent the Toilet Challenge to bring sustainable sanitation solutions to the 2.5 billion people worldwide who don’t have access to safe, inexpensive sanitation. Prof. Kara Nelson and postdoctoral researcher Temitope Ogunyoku, at the UC Berkeley Department of Environmental Engineering were recipients of grant funding through this program, with which they developed a waste disinfection process that they deemed ‘safe sludge’. ‘Safe sludge’ utilized the urea in urine to inactivate pathogens in feces – the primary cause of diseases due to fecal contamination such as Cholera and Typhoid.

The overall goal for the project’s first funding round was to develop and present a toilet that incorporated the Safe Sludge disinfection treatment method at the Bill and Melinda Gates toilet conference. This initial investigation formed the basis for further funding applications.

The Team | My Role

When the time came to design the toilet that would showcase the ‘Safe Sludge’ disinfection approach, the team sought out collaborators to help design and develop the prototype. I was involved with the design of the prototype from the initial brainstorming sessions and iterative process to material selection and shop drawings. I also developed the logo and all the presentation graphics to simply present the integral pieces of the design and sanitation process.

User Assumptions

Given the humanitarian underpinnings of this project, the end users played the most important role. The safe sludge process had already been field tested with Sanergy in Kenya, using their toilets. We knew that there were certain behaviours that increased fecal contamination in areas with sub standard human waste disposal, such as manual scavenging and association of toilets with uncleanliness and disease. The typical use case thus consisted of a family of five, with a service delivery model employed to accomodate current waste collection/ disposal practices.

Constraints

The project involved a number of constraints, some of which were requirements of the Gates Foundation and others that were necessary for the disinfection process to work. The toilet needed to treat human waste to inactivate pathogens before anyone has contact with it while also allowing for resource recovery as the sludge was typically collected and sold. It also needed to be off-the-grid, requiring no water or electricity, as well as be as user friendly and low maintenance as possible.

The Design Process

Workshops

The objective of the initial design meetings was to develop 2-4 product concepts based on the Safe Sludge disinfection treatment method. Together a group of researchers, engineers and designers brainstormed ideas for the ‘guts’ and ‘shell’ of the toilet.

Prototype development

After the workshops, we worked through a few iterations of the design with physical modelling and digital modelling in Rhino.

Design communication

I also worked on devleoping branding and signage for the project and the Gates Foundation presentation. We focused on conveying the mechanics of the prototype and disenfection system as simply as possible.

In retrospect

While the goal was to get more funding for more iterative design & testing was not met, this was still an incredible project to contribute to. Far-reaching projects such as this really do require multi-disciplinary teams of experts and this was no exception.
